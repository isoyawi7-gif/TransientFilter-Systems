Abstract

Papua New Guinea (PNG) continues to experience rapid growth in telecommunications infrastructure to support national connectivity and digital transformation. However, telecommunication systems across the country are frequently affected by unstable power conditions, including voltage fluctuations, blackouts, generator changeovers, and lightning-induced surges. These disturbances often create overvoltage and overcurrent conditions that damage sensitive equipment, resulting in operational downtime, increased maintenance costs, reduced equipment lifespan, and unreliable network performance. Existing surge protection, grounding, and earthing systems in many telecommunications sites remain insufficient or poorly maintained for PNG’s harsh operating conditions.

This research focuses on the design and evaluation of an improved surge protection system for telecommunications operating in unstable power environments. The study investigates the effects of power transitions, lightning strikes, and electrical surges on telecom infrastructure and evaluates practical surge protection, grounding, and earthing solutions to improve equipment reliability and operational stability. The effectiveness of existing Surge Protection Devices (SPDs) and the influence of grounding quality on surge energy dissipation are also examined.

The proposed methodology includes monitoring and recording surge and overcurrent events through laboratory experiments and field-based observations from telecommunications installations. Data from surge events, grounding resistance measurements, and equipment performance analysis will be used to identify system weaknesses and optimize protection methods.

Expected outcomes include reduced equipment failures, improved system reliability, enhanced operational stability, lower maintenance costs, and increased lifespan of critical telecommunications infrastructure. The study aims to provide practical protection guidelines for telecom operators and support the development of resilient telecommunications networks in Papua New Guinea.

Introduction

Telecommunications applications in Papua New Guinea is frequently affected by power instability, blackouts, and lightning strikes. During transitions between power sources, surge currents damage sensitive components, causing failures and downtime.

This research focuses on improving surge protection, grounding, and earthing systems to enhance applications reliability and reduce operational risks.

Problem Statement

Telecommunication base station applications are highly vulnerable to electrical surges caused by lightning strikes, unstable grid supply, and switching transients. In many installations, especially in regions with high lightning activity and unreliable power infrastructure, existing surge protection systems are either inadequate, poorly designed, or improperly maintained.

As a result, these surges lead to frequent equipment failures, increased operational downtime, and high maintenance and replacement costs. Despite the availability of surge protection devices, there is limited localized research on their effectiveness under real operating conditions and how they can be optimized for improved protection.

Therefore, there is a need to investigate the causes and impact of electrical surges on telecommunications applications and to develop a more reliable and efficient surge protection and filter approach suitable for such environments.


Research Questions

The Research question:

1. How can surge protection, earthing, and grounding systems be optimized to improve the reliability and operational stability of telecommunication equipment under power fluctuations and lightning-induced surges?

2. What are the effects of power transitions (main supply to generator) on telecommunication equipment, and how do these transitions contribute to surge-related failures?

3. How effective are existing surge protection devices (SPDs) in telecommunication sites in mitigating overvoltage and overcurrent conditions?

4. How does the quality of earthing and grounding systems influence the dissipation of surge energy in telecommunications infrastructure?

5. What is the impact of lightning-induced surges on telecom equipment, and how can protection systems be enhanced to withstand these events?

6. How can overcurrent events be captured, recorded, and analyzed to improve surge protection system design?

7. To what extent does improved surge protection and grounding increase the lifespan and reliability of telecommunication equipment?

8. What practical surge protection and grounding design framework can be developed for telecom sites in high-risk environments like Papua New Guinea?

Objectives

This research is guided by the following key objectives;

  • 1) Improved understanding of how power surges, generator changeovers, and lightning events affect telecommunication equipment performance and reliability. .
  • 2) Identification of weaknesses in existing surge protection, earthing, and grounding systems used in telecommunication sites.
  • 3)Development of improved surge protection and grounding design solutions suitable for unstable power environments.
  • 4)Establishment of effective methods for monitoring and recording overcurrent events for analysis and future system improvements.
  • 5)Reduction in equipment failures such as overheating, circuit damage, and unexpected shutdowns caused by electrical surges.
  • 6)Enhanced reliability and stability of telecommunication systems, leading to reduced downtime and maintenance costs.
  • 7) Extension of the operational lifespan of critical telecommunication equipment.
  • 8)Development of practical installation and maintenance guidelines for telecom operators to implement improved protection systems.
  • 9)Contribution to existing knowledge by providing field-based research data relevant to developing countries with unstable power infrastructure, such as Papua New Guinea.

Vocabulary

  • Amplitude: The maximum value of a voltage or current waveform.
  • Arrester: A device used to protect electrical systems from lightning and surge effects by diverting excess voltage to ground.
  • Bonding: The process of connecting conductive parts together to ensure electrical continuity and safety.
  • Breakdown Voltage: The voltage at which an insulating material becomes conductive.
  • Circuit: A closed path through which electric current flows.
  • Clamping Voltage: The maximum voltage that a Surge Protection Device (SPD) allows before diverting excess energy.
  • Conductor: A material that allows the flow of electric current easily.
  • Current: The flow of electric charge through a conductor, measured in amperes.
  • Earthing (Grounding): The connection of electrical systems to the earth to safely dissipate unwanted currents.
  • Energy: The capacity to do work, often measured in joules in electrical systems.
  • Frequency: The number of cycles per second of an electrical signal, measured in hertz (Hz).
  • Impulse: A sudden surge of energy or voltage occurring over a very short time.
  • Insulator: A material that resists the flow of electric current.
  • Lightning Surge: A high-energy transient caused by a lightning strike.
  • Nominal Voltage: The standard operating voltage of a system.
  • Overvoltage: A voltage higher than the normal operating level of a system.
  • Resistance: The opposition to the flow of current, measured in ohms (Ω).
  • Surge: A temporary increase in voltage above the normal level.
  • Surge Protection Device (SPD): A device that protects electrical equipment by diverting transient overvoltages to ground.
  • Transient: A short-duration spike or disturbance in voltage or current.
  • Voltage: The electrical potential difference that drives current in a circuit.
